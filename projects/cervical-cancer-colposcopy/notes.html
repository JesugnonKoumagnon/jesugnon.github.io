Project Notes · MSc Thesis

Deep Learning for Cervical Cancer Screening in Benin

A web-based diagnostic tool that classifies colposcopy images into six diagnostic categories in real time, and shows clinicians where the lesion is — built and validated with the Departmental University Hospital of Parakou (CHUD-B/A).

Read full thesis (PDF) ↗

The problem

Cervical cancer is the second most common cancer among women in Benin and the leading cause of female cancer deaths, with roughly 1,000+ new cases and ~700 deaths each year. There is no national screening program, trained colposcopists are scarce, and colposcopy itself is subjective — diagnostic accuracy depends heavily on the examining clinician. The goal of the thesis was to build an AI tool that standardizes reading of colposcopy images and works on standard hardware in a low-resource clinic.

Key result

Result

Of three deep-learning approaches, a VGG-19 transfer-learning model performed best at 89.17% accuracy (precision 89.28%, recall 89.17%, F1 89.22%) across six classes. It was deployed in a multilingual web app (EmiloVision Benin) that accepts an uploaded image or a live camera capture and returns a classification, per-class confidence scores, and a Grad-CAM overlay marking the affected area.

Data

494 colposcopy images were collected during routine screening at CHUD-B/A Parakou and annotated by local oncology clinicians, then balanced to 1,800 images (300 per class) through augmentation. All images were anonymized before analysis.

Models compared

Three approaches were trained on the same 70/15/15 split and evaluated on a held-out test set.

  • U-Net + SVM — segments the cervix, extracts 90 radiomic features, reduces with PCA, classifies with an RBF-kernel SVM. Lightest and fastest (≈3.5 images/sec); weakest on intermediate lesions (CIN1 vs CIN2).
  • VGG-19 transfer learning — ImageNet backbone with a custom head, fine-tuned in two phases. Best overall, and strongest on the clinically important CIN classes. Selected for deployment.
  • CYENET — a specialized attention-based architecture. Excelled on benign cases (metaplasia and ectropion, F1 96%) but weaker on CIN grades.

Why VGG-19 was chosen

The ~4-point accuracy edge over CYENET translates to roughly 12 additional correct classifications per 270 test cases — meaningful in a diagnostic setting — while keeping precision and recall balanced, which limits both missed lesions and false alarms.

The deployed tool

  • React front-end + FastAPI back-end; runs on a standard browser, no specialized software to install
  • Two input modes: upload a colposcopy image, or capture one live through the interface
  • Returns the predicted class, confidence across all six categories, and a Grad-CAM heatmap of the affected region
  • Four-language interface — French, English, Fon, and Yoruba — for the local clinical context
  • Access is restricted to approved researchers; outputs are explicitly not a substitute for clinical diagnosis at this stage

Limitations

The original dataset was small (494 images) and single-center; augmentation balances classes but cannot replicate real clinical variability. Training ran on standard CPU hardware, constraining model size and tuning. No external validation on data from other hospitals was performed — the main priority for future work, alongside expanding the dataset across multiple Benin sites.
