The Impact of Business Training on Firm Performance

A firm-level panel study (2010–2019, ~498 firms) evaluating how a targeted management-training program affected revenue, sales, employment, and labour productivity — using difference-in-differences with two-way fixed effects, an event-study check on parallel trends, and a regression-discontinuity design around the program's 100-employee eligibility threshold.

Read full report (PDF) ↗ R code ↗

The question

A management-training program launched in January 2013, open to firms with 100 or fewer employees. Did it actually improve firm performance — and through which channel: by growing the workforce, or by making existing operations more productive? The 100-employee eligibility cutoff and the voluntary take-up among eligible firms together create the variation needed for credible causal inference.

Key finding

Result

Trained firms saw substantial gains in financial performance — revenue up roughly 38%, sales up 22%, and revenue per worker up 38% — while employment was essentially unchanged. The effect runs through productivity, not headcount: training made firms more efficient rather than simply larger.

Method

ComponentDetail
DataThree firm-level sources merged into a balanced panel: firm information (498 firms, sectors), aggregate daily sales (56,646 observations), and monthly operational data (employment, wage bill, revenue, participation), Jan 2010 – Dec 2019
CleaningHarmonized inconsistent date formats (DMY vs MDY) with a custom parser, standardized firm identifiers (trimmed, uppercased, despaced), and performed a three-way merge to firm–month level
Main modelDifference-in-differences with firm and time fixed effects, comparing participants vs non-participants before and after the 2013 launch
Parallel trendsEvent-study specification — pre-period coefficients near zero, supporting the identifying assumption
RDDSharp regression-discontinuity around the 100-employee eligibility cutoff, as an independent identification strategy
Take-upVoluntary participation among eligible firms handled through an instrumental-variables approach

Robustness

  • Event-study plots for revenue, sales, and employment confirm no meaningful pre-trends before 2013
  • Regression-discontinuity around the 100-employee threshold corroborates the DiD direction independent of the parallel-trends assumption
  • Placebo panel and clustered standard errors guard against spurious significance and serial correlation
